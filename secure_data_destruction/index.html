Why destroy hard drives and SSDs?

Companies go to great lengths to protect their data from network intrusions, hacking, viruses, and theft. However, many fail to protect against the disastrous consequences of data security breaches from stored, stockpiled, donated, resold, discarded, and recycled magnetic and solid-state media that they thought was deleted, erased or secured through overwriting or encryption technology.

This is an enormous, but often overlooked source of data leakage

Defective and retired data storage devices (hard disks, tapes, SSDs, pen drives), once removed from servers, storage systems, desktops, laptops etc. present a potent and potentially massive source of data leakage. Organizations can get severly penalized for data leakage or current or even old data.

To protect your company and ensure compliance with data protection laws, it is essential to securely and completely destroy unwanted data that may reside on retired storage devices.

How are your hard drives destroyed?

Degaussing

Data is stored in the magnetic media, such as hard drives, floppy disks, and magnetic tape, by making very small areas called magnetic domains change their magnetic alignment to be in the direction of an applied magnetic field. This phenomenon occurs in much the same way a compass needle points in the direction of the Earth's magnetic field. Degaussing, commonly called erasure, leaves the domains in random patterns with no preference to orientation, thereby rendering previous data unrecoverable. There are some domains whose magnetic alignment is not randomized after degaussing. The information these domains represent is commonly called magnetic remanence or remanent magnetization. Proper degaussing will ensure there is insufficient magnetic remanence to reconstruct the data.

Physical destruction

Disk destroyers deliver approximately 15,500 pounds of crushing power to hard drives. This ensures that storage media is completely and irreversibly destroyed.
